What a Judgment Means

Congratulations on winning your lawsuit. That took hard work, patience, and determination, and it deserves to be celebrated.

A judgment is powerful because it is the court’s official order saying one person owes another money. The person who won is called the judgment creditor, and the person who lost is called the judgment debtor.

That means the court has recognized a legal debt, and the judgment creditor now has access to powerful collection tools to discover income, property, and other assets that may be used to satisfy the judgment.

How Collection Works

1

Find out what exists

Every case is different. Some judgment debtors have wages, some have bank accounts, and some have property or business assets. The first step is figuring out what can actually be collected [web:1].

2

Use the court’s collection tools

Washington law allows post-judgment discovery and enforcement methods that help identify assets and income. These tools can help reveal where money or property may be held [web:1][web:154].

3

Turn assets into payment

Once non-exempt assets are identified, collection can move through garnishment, liens, execution, sheriff sales, or other lawful enforcement methods depending on the facts of the case [web:1][web:9].

What Can Be Found

Judgment debtors may own many different kinds of assets, and each case requires a different strategy. Possible assets can include real estate, vehicles, intellectual property, business assets, and personal property.
